Module 2
Mishimoto Diesel Radiators

OEM cooling systems aren't built for the demands of towing, tuning, or hard use. Here's what Mishimoto does differently — and why it matters.

Why OEM Radiators Fall Short
  • Plastic end tanks crack under thermal cycling and high-pressure spikes
  • Single-pass or minimal-pass cores limit coolant dwell time and heat transfer
  • Reduced core thickness optimized for cost, not thermal capacity
  • No design margin for tuned engines producing excess heat
  • No accommodation for added EGTs from towing or regen cycles
Mishimoto Construction Highlights
  • All-Aluminum Core & End Tanks — Eliminates the plastic-to-aluminum joint (most common failure point)
  • High-Density Tube-and-Fin Core — Up to 40% more surface area than stock
  • Dual/Triple-Pass Flow — Extended coolant contact time for maximum heat rejection
  • Direct-Fit OEM Compatibility — Factory mounting points, hose connections
📐 Technical Detail

Mishimoto's brazed aluminum construction eliminates the crimped plastic tank joint found on OEM radiators. This joint is the primary failure point on high-mileage diesel trucks — particularly on Duramax, Powerstroke, and Cummins platforms where coolant pressure spikes during towing can exceed OEM tank design limits.

AFE Power Charge Pipe Kits

The charge pipe system is only as strong as its weakest link. AFE BladeRunner aluminum kits eliminate OEM plastic failure points and improve airflow efficiency across the board.

OEM Charge Pipe Weaknesses
  • Thin-wall plastic construction prone to cracking at coupler connection points
  • Restrictive bends and smaller ID that limit airflow at higher boost levels
  • Rubber couplers that harden and crack from heat cycling and high EGTs
  • No provision for aftermarket IAT or BOV ports
  • Platform specifics: Ford 6.7L lower pipe, Ram 6.7L Y-bridge, GM LML boost tube
AFE BladeRunner Aluminum
  • 6061-T6 aluminum — 0.080" wall, lightweight, corrosion resistant

  • High-temp silicone couplers — rated to 350°F continuous service
  • Stainless T-bolt clamps — replaces all OEM spring clamps
  • Pre-tapped sensor bungs — factory IAT sensor locations included
  • Finish: Polished or powder-coated black available

Handling Common Objections
"The OEM part is still working fine — why replace it?"
The OEM intercooler was designed for stock power levels and ideal conditions. Most intercooler failures are gradual — customers don't realize they're losing 15–20 horsepower from heat soak until after they upgrade. The question isn't whether the OEM part is working — it's whether it's working well enough for how this customer actually uses their truck.
"That seems expensive for just a pipe/radiator."
Compare it to a roadside breakdown from a cracked plastic end tank, or engine repair from sustained heat damage on a long tow. These parts protect a $40,000–$80,000 vehicle. The Mishimoto lifetime warranty means this is a buy-once component. Divide the price over the life of the truck — it's a very small number per year.
"I just have a stock tune — do I really need this?"
Many customers see improvements even on stock tunes, especially on high-mileage trucks where OEM systems have degraded. More importantly, if they're considering a tune now or in the future, these upgrades need to be in place first. Tuning without cooling upgrades is where problems start — getting these done now sets them up to tune safely when they're ready.
